1. EU AI ACT OBLIGATION — Article 50 Transparency Duty
================================================================================

Concrete obligation triggered:

  Article 50 of the EU AI Act requires that providers of AI systems intended
  to interact directly with natural persons design and develop the system in
  such a way that the natural persons are informed that they are interacting
  with an AI system, unless this is obvious from the circumstances.

  The Communicator agent generates push notifications, email subject lines,
  and email body text that a customer would reasonably believe was written by
  a human marketing employee. The messages are personalised with the customer's
  name, favourite product, and preferred store — creating the impression of
  individual human attention. This is precisely the scenario Article 50
  contemplates: an AI system producing outputs that mimic human communication
  without making the AI nature obvious to the recipient.

  The pipeline does NOT fall under Annex III high-risk classification.
  Recommending coffee offers to loyalty members is not biometric
  categorisation, critical infrastructure management, educational evaluation,
  employment decision-making, essential service access, law enforcement,
  migration management, or judicial administration. The system is in the
  "limited risk" category, subject primarily to transparency obligations
  rather than conformity assessments or CE marking requirements.

How I would meet this obligation:

  1. EMAIL FOOTER DISCLOSURE: Every email sent by the Communicator includes a
     single line at the bottom, below the unsubscribe link:
     "This message was personalised using automated systems based on your
     purchase history and preferences."

  2. APP PRIVACY SETTINGS: The Drift Coffee app privacy section includes a
     clear statement: "We use automated systems to personalise your offers
     and recommendations based on your order history. You can disable
     personalisation at any time in Settings."

  3. ONBOARDING DISCLOSURE: When a user signs up for the loyalty programme,
     the terms include a specific line: "You may receive personalised
     communications generated by automated systems. These are based on your
     purchase activity and can be turned off in Settings."

  The obligation is proportional to the risk level. A full "conversation with
  an AI" disclosure (as would be required for a chatbot) is not necessary here
  because the AI is generating one-way marketing messages, not engaging in
  interactive dialogue. The footer disclosure is sufficient to meet the "informed
  that they are interacting with an AI system" requirement without degrading
  the user experience with intrusive pop-ups or banners on every message.

2. GDPR OBLIGATION — Lawful Basis and Data Subject Access Request
================================================================================

Personal data processed by the pipeline:

  | Category                    | Specific Data Fields                           |
  |-----------------------------|------------------------------------------------|
  | Identity data               | First name, email address, loyalty member ID   |
  | Transaction data            | Individual SKU-level purchase history, visit   |
  |                             | timestamps, basket composition, spend per visit|
  | Location data               | Store visited, geolocation at time of visit,   |
  |                             | postcode (for residential area mapping)        |
  | Behavioural data            | App login frequency, session duration, push    |
  |                             | notification status, payment method saved      |
  | Segment assignment          | Researcher-generated behavioural segment label |
  |                             | (e.g., "Commuter", "Weekend Explorer")         |
  | Derived inferences          | Churn risk, favourite product, preferred store,|
  |                             | visit cadence patterns                         |

Lawful basis:

  The primary lawful basis is legitimate interest under Article 6(1)(f) GDPR.
  Drift Coffee has a legitimate business interest in re-engaging inactive
  loyalty programme members to sustain the commercial viability of the
  programme. These individuals voluntarily enrolled in the loyalty programme,
  provided their data, and agreed to the programme terms, which include
  receiving offers and communications.

  The legitimate interest assessment (LIA) balancing test:
    - Purpose: Re-engage dormant loyalty members with personalised offers
      based on their own purchase history
    - Necessity: Personalisation requires processing purchase history;
      generic mass marketing would not achieve the same re-engagement
      outcome and would itself be more intrusive
    - Balancing: The data subject has an existing commercial relationship
      with Drift Coffee, receives messages about products they have previously
      purchased, can opt out at any time, and the data processing does not
      involve sensitive categories (special category data under Article 9)
    - Safeguards: No automated decision-making with legal or similarly
      significant effects (Article 22 does not apply — a coffee offer is not
      a decision producing legal effects). Push notification opt-in provides
      an additional consent layer for that specific channel.

  For push notifications specifically, the basis is consent (Article 6(1)(a)),
  collected at the OS level via iOS/Android notification permission prompts.

How I would handle a Data Subject Access Request (DSAR):

  A Drift Coffee loyalty member has the right under Article 15 GDPR to
  request confirmation of whether their personal data is being processed,
  access to that data, and information about the processing.

  DSAR handling procedure:

  1. SUBMISSION: The user submits a DSAR via email to
     dpo@driftcoffee.com or through the in-app "My Data" portal.

  2. VERIFICATION: Drift Coffee verifies the requester's identity by
     matching the email address to the loyalty account and sending a
     confirmation code to the registered email or phone number.

  3. RESPONSE (within 30 days, per Article 12(3) GDPR):
     - A structured export containing:
       * All purchase history (timestamp, store, SKU, spend)
       * All personal data fields stored in the loyalty profile
       * The user's behavioural segment assignment
       * A log of all push notifications and emails sent to the user
       * The personalisation rules that triggered each message
     - Format: Machine-readable (JSON)
     - Accompanying explanation of:
       * Purposes of processing
       * Categories of data processed
       * Recipients (no third parties — data is processed in-house)
       * Retention period (data retained for the life of the loyalty
         membership + 12 months after account closure)
       * Rights to rectification (Article 16), erasure (Article 17),
         restriction (Article 18), and objection (Article 21)

  4. ERASURE: If the user requests deletion under Article 17, Drift Coffee
     deletes the loyalty profile, purchase history, and segment assignment
     within 30 days. Aggregated/anonymised store-level analytics that
     included this user's purchases are not affected (recital 26: anonymised
     data is not personal data).

  The pipeline does not involve automated decision-making with legal or
  similarly significant effects (Article 22), so no additional explainability
  or human intervention mechanism is required beyond the standard DSAR
  response.

3. CUSTOMER TRUST AND PERCEPTION
================================================================================

  Drift Coffee customers would perceive this system positively when it gets
  the personalisation right — a morning commuter receiving a push for their
  exact usual latte at their exact usual store before 9am feels seen and
  valued, not surveilled. That perception would flip to discomfort if the
  system overreaches: referencing a store the customer visited once six
  months ago, or sending a push at 7:30am to a user who has push disabled,
  would signal carelessness rather than care. Trust in this system therefore
  depends on two things: data accuracy (the personalisation must be correct)
  and restraint (the system must know when to stay silent). A customer who
  can see that the app knows their favourite drink, uses it to save them
  money, and never sends a notification they did not ask for will trust the
  system. A customer who receives an irrelevant offer or an unwanted
  notification will distrust the entire brand — not just the AI.
